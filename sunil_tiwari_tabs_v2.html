I am a Senior Lecturer in Operations Management at the University of Bristol Business School, where I also serve as Programme Director of the MSc Business Analytics. Before joining Bristol, I held academic positions at ESSCA School of Management in France (2021–2023) and the National University of Singapore (2017–2021), and was Visiting Faculty at the Institute for Manufacturing (IfM), University of Cambridge.

My research sits at the intersection of operations, supply chain management, and emerging technologies. I examine how organisations can design smarter, fairer, and more resilient supply chains — from healthcare logistics and sustainability governance to the strategic implications of AI, blockchain, and robotics.

Over the years I have led industrial projects with global organisations including Procter & Gamble, National Healthcare Group (Singapore), SingHealth, ST Logistics, Micron Technology, SATS Ltd., Singapore Post, POS Indonesia, and Huawei. My work has been published in leading journals including the European Journal of Operational Research, International Journal of Production Economics, Transportation Research Part E, IEEE Transactions on Engineering Management, and Annals of Operations Research.

I serve as Department Editor of IEEE Transactions on Engineering Management and Associate Editor for the Journal of the Operational Research Society, International Transactions in Operational Research, and Supply Chain Analytics.

Research

My research examines how organisations design, govern, and transform supply chains — with a focus on what makes them fairer, more resilient, and better equipped for an uncertain world. I combine analytical modelling, game theory, empirical analysis, and simulation with field work to tackle problems that are both theoretically rich and practically consequential. My work spans six interconnected streams.

01

Buyer–Supplier Relationships, Contracts & Strategic Interactions

At the heart of every supply chain is a relationship — between a buyer and a supplier, a manufacturer and a retailer, a platform and its sellers. These relationships are governed, explicitly or implicitly, by contracts: wholesale price agreements, two-part tariffs, buyback provisions, revenue-sharing clauses. My work in this stream asks how those contracts should be designed, what strategic behaviour they induce, and how information asymmetry and uncertainty change the picture.

In a series of papers with Indranil Biswas and co-authors, I have developed analytical frameworks for contract design in Industry 4.0 settings. One paper examines how a manufacturer should decide whether to introduce an Industry 4.0 innovative product line alongside a traditional product — characterising the critical market potential threshold at which the innovative product overtakes the traditional one in sales, even when sold at a premium. We show that under both wholesale and two-part tariff contracts, the cessation points and market potential thresholds move in opposite directions — a counter-intuitive but practically important result for firms navigating the transition to smart manufacturing.

"There exists a critical market potential threshold above which the sale of the Industry 4.0 innovative product is higher than that of the traditional product, even when the former is sold at a higher price."

A second line of work examines multi-echelon supply chain coordination — how to sequence contracts and design cut-off policies across multiple tiers so that the whole supply chain achieves near-optimal performance. More recently, I have turned to platform financing and product returns: cash-constrained sellers on platforms like Amazon and Alibaba face a tension between accepting platform credit and the platform's return policies that can undermine their profitability. Ongoing work also examines ex-ante incentives versus ex-post penalties in partner selection under commitment uncertainty — a question that arises whenever a firm must choose a supplier before observing whether that supplier will honour its commitments.

02

Sustainability, Inclusion & Ethical Governance in Supply Chains

Global supply chains carry with them histories of exploitation, environmental harm, and structural inequality. My work in this stream takes seriously the idea that operations researchers have something important to say about how supply chains can be made more responsible — not through wishful thinking, but through rigorous analysis of incentives, governance structures, and the conditions under which ethical outcomes actually emerge.

One major thread concerns modern slavery and forced labour in agrifood supply chains. Working with colleagues at UIR Rabat and EDHEC, I have developed frameworks for understanding how governance configurations interact to either suppress or inadvertently enable labour exploitation in complex, informal supply chains, particularly in Africa. A recent paper in the Journal of Business Research uses Extreme Value Theory to model modern slavery as a tail risk — treating the financial exposure of firms in ESG-vulnerable sectors (agrifood, extractives, financial services) as analogous to catastrophic loss events in risk modelling.

"Modern slavery is not merely an ethical failure but a quantifiable financial tail risk — one that ESG frameworks, properly constructed, can price and mitigate."

A complementary thread examines how board-level gender diversity shapes firms' environmental supplier governance. Using large-sample archival data, this work shows that companies with greater female board representation adopt more stringent supplier sustainability requirements — not simply because of regulatory pressure, but because diverse boards change the information-processing and incentive structures that drive CSR strategy. I am also engaged in work on circular economy transitions and the adoption of Industry 4.0 technologies for sustainability in the textile sector, combining empirical SEM analysis with optimisation frameworks.

03

Healthcare Supply Chains: Efficiency, Automation & Resilience

Healthcare supply chains are among the most consequential and least well-understood in operations management. Failures — a stockout of surgical consumables, a delay in medication delivery — translate directly into patient harm. At the same time, healthcare institutions operate under relentless pressure to reduce costs, making the tension between service level and efficiency unusually acute.

My engagement with healthcare operations began in Singapore, where I led a large multi-year programme of research and industrial projects with the National Healthcare Group, SingHealth, and other institutions, funded by the National Robotics Programme Office (S$3M). The core question was: how can hospitals redesign their internal logistics systems, replacing manual material handling with Automated Guided Vehicles (AGVs) and robotics, without disrupting patient care during the transition? My team developed simulation-based digital twins of hospital supply chains in Simio — models that replicated the real-time flow of materials through wards, pharmacies, and sterilisation units. Optimising these simulations, we identified the optimal number and routing of AGVs for each clinical setting and quantified the productivity gains from automation.

"The future hospital is not just a building with better equipment — it is a logistics network, continuously optimised, that redirects human attention from material handling to patient care."

A second project, funded by AI Singapore (S$250K with National Innovation Health Centre), developed machine learning algorithms for medical consumables prediction — using inpatient health records to anticipate what consumables each patient is likely to need during their stay. This resulted in a web-based deployment tool built in Dash-Plotly. More recent theoretical work examines IoT-enabled inventory systems for perishable healthcare goods, and a working paper in progress explores how blockchain-enabled data-sharing platforms and large language models might be integrated to support clinical analytics in high-equivocality settings.

04

Disruptive Technologies: Blockchain, AI & Digital Transformation

My approach to disruptive technologies is deliberately sceptical in the best sense: I ask not whether a technology is exciting, but under what conditions it actually improves supply chain performance, and for whom. This leads to work that is more technically precise and more practically useful than general advocacy for adoption.

My work on blockchain technology spans empirical and theoretical approaches. A widely cited paper in Transportation Research Part E (132+ Scopus citations) examined logistics company managers' perspectives on blockchain adoption for 3PL operations, documenting the practical barriers — interoperability, cost, data quality, and competitor reluctance — that prevent blockchain from delivering its theoretical promise. We used these insights to develop a decision roadmap firms can use to evaluate whether blockchain is appropriate for their context. A new paper in the International Journal of Production Research develops a dynamic Stackelberg differential game modelling how blockchain-enabled transparency affects investment decisions in low-carbon supply chains, revealing a fundamental tension: transparency can reduce information asymmetry but also changes strategic dynamics in ways that may reduce participation incentives.

A separate thread examines generative AI and platform economics. A 2025 paper in IJPE is among the first to formally model how Gen-AI might promote community group buying, using a tripartite evolutionary game. Ongoing work examines how AI transparency requirements interact with consumer data monetisation and privacy regulation. A 2024 paper in IJPE on supply chain digitalization and COVID-19 uses institutional and contingency theory to explain why some firms were better positioned to digitalize quickly when the pandemic struck — showing that digital transformation is shaped profoundly by institutional environment and prior relational investments, not technology alone.

05

Climate Risk, Extreme Weather & Supply Chain Resilience

Climate change is already disrupting supply chains in ways that operations management theory is only beginning to catch up with. Understanding how supply chains should be designed and governed to withstand these shocks — and how the cost of climate resilience should be shared across partners — is one of the most pressing questions in the field.

A recent paper in the European Journal of Operational Research (2025) takes a game-theoretic approach: we consider a supplier deciding whether to invest in climate-specific assets or generic assets, and model the interaction with a buyer's ordering behaviour using a dynamic game. Strikingly, when the probability of disruption is high, the supplier actually prefers generic investment — because the cost of climate-specific assets becomes difficult to recoup through higher prices. But when the probability is lower, a proactive supplier invests in climate-specific assets, and this investment itself serves as a credible signal to the buyer about the supplier's type — allowing buyers to identify reliable suppliers in climate-stressed environments without direct observation. A pooling equilibrium exists where all supplier types choose climate-specific investment, sustained through reciprocal behaviour.

A companion paper on stock reallocation for repairable service parts under extreme weather disruptions develops optimal reallocation policies for firms managing service networks where a regional depot can be knocked out entirely — directly applicable to utility companies, airlines, and defence contractors. More broadly, a paper in IEEE Transactions on Engineering Management applies Information Processing Theory to how firms use big data analytics during crises, showing that the value of analytics depends critically on the equivocality of the situation, not just information volume.

06

Pricing, Inventory Optimisation & Data-Driven Operations

Much of my earlier work was grounded in inventory theory — a classical area of operations research concerned with how firms should manage stock given uncertain demand, lead time variability, deterioration, and the availability of credit. While more technical in character than my current streams, it forms the analytical foundation for everything I do, and several papers from this period continue to be heavily cited.

A characteristic feature is its integration of real-world frictions into models that had previously assumed them away — for instance, two-level partial trade credits in joint pricing and inventory models for deteriorating items, where buyers receive credit from suppliers and then extend credit to their own customers, creating a nested financial structure that interacts with inventory decisions in non-trivial ways.

A more recent strand is data-driven and behavioural. A paper in the Journal of the Operational Research Society (2025) examines dynamic pricing with demand learning, incorporating sticky fairness concerns: consumers who perceive prices as unfair remember and punish the retailer in future periods, creating a dynamic in which short-term exploitation of demand uncertainty undermines long-run profitability. The model produces a data-driven pricing policy that navigates the exploitation-exploration trade-off while remaining robust to consumer fairness perceptions. Methodologically, this stream draws on optimal control, dynamic programming, stochastic modelling, and evolutionary game theory.
